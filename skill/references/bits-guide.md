# Bit Selection Guide

## End Mill Types

### Upcut Spiral
- **What it does:** Pulls chips UP and out of the cut
- **Use for:** Pocketing, through-cuts in solid wood, any deep cut where chip evacuation matters
- **Avoid when:** Cutting veneered plywood (causes tearout/fuzzy top edge), thin material that can lift
- **Sizes to own:** 1/8" and 1/4" single-flute (starter), 1/4" two-flute (once comfortable)

### Downcut Spiral
- **What it does:** Pushes chips DOWN into the cut
- **Use for:** Clean top surface on plywood, veneers, shallow engraving, thin material, signage
- **Avoid when:** Deep pockets (chips pack in the cut, re-cut, generate heat)
- **Sizes to own:** 1/8" and 1/4" single-flute

### Compression
- **What it does:** Upcut at the tip + downcut above. Gives clean edges on BOTH faces.
- **Use for:** Through-cuts in plywood and laminate where both sides must be clean
- **IMPORTANT:** Only works when cutting deeper than the upcut zone (typically >5mm). For shallow cuts it acts like a pure upcut and tears the top.
- **Sizes to own:** 1/4" two-flute

### O-Flute (Single Flute)
- **What it does:** One cutting edge with a large flute valley for excellent chip evacuation
- **Use for:** Plastics (acrylic, HDPE, polycarbonate), aluminum, any material that melts or gums
- **Why:** The large flute valley clears chips fast, preventing heat buildup and re-welding
- **Sizes to own:** 1/8" and 1/4"

### Straight Flute
- **What it does:** No helix, straight cutting edge
- **Use for:** Foam, soft materials
- **Avoid when:** Hardwood or any material needing good chip evacuation

## Specialty Bits

### V-Bits
- **Angles:** 30, 60, or 90 degrees (most common: 60 and 90)
- **Use for:** V-carving, lettering, engraving, chamfering, inlay work
- **60 degree:** Narrower, deeper cuts; better for fine text and detail
- **90 degree:** Wider, shallower cuts; better for bold lettering and large designs
- **Tip:** The tip is fragile. Plunge slowly and never crash into the workpiece.

### Ball Nose
- **Use for:** 3D carving, contour finishing, topographic maps, sculpted surfaces
- **How it works:** Round tip follows 3D contours smoothly
- **Sizes:** 1/8" and 1/4" cover most hobby work
- **Note:** Requires 3D toolpaths (not just 2.5D)

### Surfacing / Fly Cutter Bit
- **Size:** 1" to 2" (25-50mm) diameter
- **Use for:** Flattening your spoilboard, facing large flat areas
- **Settings:** Very slow feed (500-800 mm/min), shallow depth (0.5-1mm per pass), dial 1-2
- **Note:** Only use for surfacing operations, never for profiling or pocketing

## Recommended Starter Kit

If you're just starting out, get these bits first:

| Priority | Bit | Size | Why |
|----------|-----|------|-----|
| 1 | Single-flute upcut spiral | 1/8" (3.175mm) | V1 Engineering's recommended first bit. Most versatile. |
| 2 | Single-flute upcut spiral | 1/4" (6.35mm) | Faster material removal for larger work |
| 3 | 90-degree V-bit | any | For lettering and V-carving |
| 4 | Downcut spiral | 1/8" (3.175mm) | Clean top edges on plywood |
| 5 | Surfacing bit | 1" (25mm) | For flattening your spoilboard |

**Buy carbide, not HSS.** Carbide bits stay sharp much longer and give cleaner cuts. They cost more but break less often (assuming correct feeds & speeds).

**Buy extras of 1/8" bits.** They are the most commonly broken bit for beginners. Having spares means you don't stop a project to wait for shipping.

## Collet Compatibility (Kress 800 FME-Q)

Your Kress comes with an 8mm collet. You need additional collets for common bit shank sizes:

| Bit Shank | Collet Needed | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|-------|
| 1/8" (3.175mm) | 3.175mm collet | Most common hobby bit size |
| 1/4" (6.35mm) | 6.35mm collet | Larger bits for faster cuts |
| 6mm | 6mm collet | Metric equivalent, common in EU |
| 8mm | Included | Larger bits, surfacing |

**NEVER use a 6mm collet for a 1/4" (6.35mm) bit or vice versa.** The 0.35mm difference means the collet won't grip properly, and the bit can slip or fly out. Always match the collet to the exact shank diameter.

## When to Replace a Bit

- Edges are burning even at correct feeds & speeds
- Cut quality suddenly degrades (fuzzy edges, rough bottom)
- Visible chips or wear on the cutting edges (use a magnifier)
- Bit makes a different sound than when new (higher pitch = dull)
- Excessive heat buildup during normal cuts
